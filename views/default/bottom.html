    Does a lender's occupation affect the types of projects they support? We'll focus on agriculture/food projects, using this visualization of Kiva.org's April data on U.S. lenders for 2007-2012. Click on the next arrow button that appears in the upper right corner of this box once data is loaded.

    First, let's take a look at an occupation that is generally considered to have no ties to agriculture/food businesses: photographer. Out of all projects U.S. photographers supported, agriculture/food projects made up 40%-50%, around the total average for U.S. lenders.

    In 2007, photographers supported projects in most continents (hover over circles on the map to see loan/lender details - note: the area of each loan circle is proportional to the toal dollar amount of the loan, which can include money from lenders not currently shown on the map).

    As Kiva gained traction in 2008, more projects were supported in general. The proportion of types of projects, however, remained the same in general.

    The number of projects and size ($) of loans kept increasing overall in 2009. Still, nothing special with respect to agriculture/food projects.

    The trend continued for U.S. photographers through 2012.

    Farmers who lend through Kiva have the option to help small business owners with the same professional calling as them (agriculture/food). On the one hand, they might be more likely to support them due to feelings of solidarity or expert knowledge in the industry. On the other hand, Kiva might represent an opportunity to be involved in areas they are not exposed to in daily life. Once the farmer data is loaded, hit next to find out which was the case.

    Farmers are the lender group most likely to support entrepreneurs in agriculture and food business. This is the case in every continent.

    This pattern is consistent for all time periods. Indeed, these lenders help put food on the world's table in multiple ways.

    For U.S. farmers, their daily mission to provide food for others extends to their lending activities through Kiva.org. What other interesting patterns can we find?
